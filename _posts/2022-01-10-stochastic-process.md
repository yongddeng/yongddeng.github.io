# Stochastic Process
---
The notes 10, 11, and 12 are based on Patrick Billingsley's [Probability and Measure]() and J.L. Doob's [Stochastic Processes](). A stochastic process is a vast object that can be grouped into various categories, thus I would properly read them later (i.e. with an extra [resource](https://www.stat.cmu.edu/~cshalizi/754/)) and just focus on discrete time processes for now.


## I
---
Suppose $T$ is a [totally ordered set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_order) which denotes time, $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is a probability space, and $(\mathbb{S}, \mathcal{S})$ is a state space. A [stochastic process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stochastic_process) is a sequence $X = (X\_{t})\_{t \in T}$ of random variables $X_{t}: \Omega \to \mathbb{S}$, where $X\_{t}$ may not be well-defined at some $t$. A discrete and a continuous stochastic process can be index by $T = \mathbb{N}$ and $T = [0,\infty)$, respectively, and a state space is often set to be $\mathbb{S} \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ **(#1)**. Note that $X$ is a function of $\omega \in \Omega$ and $t \in T$, i.e. $X:T\times\Omega\to\mathbb{R}$, and thus notations such as $X(t)$, $X\_{t}(\omega)$, or $X(t,\omega)$ are all used for $X\_{t}$. A function $X(\,\cdot, \omega): T \to \mathbb{R}$ with any $\omega \in \Omega$ is a [sample path](https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/1472068/what-is-a-sample-path-of-a-stochastic-process) or realisation of $X$, and a $\sigma$-algebra $\sigma(\mathbb{R})$ over $T$ contains full of pdfs and/or pmfs.

We can access the full information about $X$ from the $\sigma$-algebra generated by $\bigcup_{t=1}^{\infty}X_t$ assumed that $X$ is running in the infinite time. That is, every information relevant to $X$ on some finite time interval $T = \lbrace 1, 2, \dots, n \rbrace$ will be conveyed by the $\sigma$-algebra generated by the set $\bigcap\_{t=1}^{n}\lbrace \omega: X\_{t}(\omega) \leq x\_{t} \rbrace$. In particular, for all $x = (x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the joint cdf of $X$ is calculated by $F_X(x) = \mathbb{P}((-\infty, x])$ and a collection of $F_X(\cdot)$ is called a [finite-dimensional distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite-dimensional_distribution) (f.d.d.) of $X$. In other words, a f.d.d. is the dist. of an $\mathbb{R}^n$-valued random vector $X = (X\_{1}, X\_{2}, \dots, X\_{n})$ which corresponds to a product measure whenever the selected timestamps yield ind. random variables.

## II
---
If a f.d.d. of $X$ is invariant under time translations, thus $F_{(X\_{1}, \cdots, X\_{n})}(x\_1, \dots, x\_n) = F_{(X\_{1+\tau}, \cdots, X\_{n+\tau})}(x\_{1+\tau}, \dots, x\_{n+\tau})$ for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$, then $X$ is said to be [strict-sense stationary]() (sss). Instead, if $n \in \lbrace 1, 2, \cdots, N \rbrace$, then $X$ is [$N$-th order stationary]() **(#2)**. We however resort to a weaker condition since the ss-stationarity is restrictive. Formally, define the 1st moment $\mu_X(t) = \operatorname{E}X_t$, and the 2nd central moment $\gamma_{XX}(t,s) = \operatorname{Cov}(X\_{t}, X\_{s})$, where $t,s \in T$. If $\mu_X(t) = \mu_X$ and $\gamma_{XX}(t, s) = \gamma_{XX}(\tau, 0)$ with $s \leq t$ and $\tau = t - s$, then $X$ is said to be [weak-sense stationary]() (wss). Notice that the [autocovariance]() yields the [autocorrelation]() $\rho_{XX}(t, s) = \gamma_{XX}(t,s)[\sigma_{X}(t)\sigma_{X}(s)]^{-1}$.

Let $X$ be stationary, $\bar{x} = {1\over{2T}}\int_{-T}^{T} X(t)\, \mathrm{d}t$ be the [time average](), and $\mu\_{X} = \operatorname{E}X(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} zf\_{X\_t}(z)\,\mathrm{d}z$ be the [ensemble average](). If it is true that $\lim\_{T\to\infty}\bar{x} = \mu\_X$, then such a process is called [mean-ergodic](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=k6y2kzayV6A#t=1520), and we can evaluate the statistical properties of $X$ by only using a single long sample path (i.e. observing all possible samples at some fixed time $t$ may be difficult). On the other hand, let $\bar{\gamma}\_{XX}(\tau) = {1\over{2T}}\int\_{-T}^{T}\,(X(t+\tau)-\mu_X)(X(t)-\mu_X) \, \mathrm{d}t$ be the [time average estimate](). If $\lim\_{T\to\infty}\bar{\gamma}\_{XX}(\tau) = \gamma\_{XX}(\tau)$, then such a process is called [autocovariance-ergodic](). A process that is both mean-ergodic and autocovariance-ergodic is called wide-sense ergodic **(#3)**.
 
For every $s, t \in T$ with $s \leq t$, a stochastic process $X$ has [independent increments]() (i.e. differences) if $X\_t - X\_s$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}\_s$, and [stationary increments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stationary_increments) if $F\_{X\_t - X\_s} = F\_{X\_{t-s} - X\_0}$. Furthermore, $X$ has independent and stationary increments if and only if $X$ is a partial sum process consists of a sequence $U = (U\_{t})\_{t \in T}$ of i.i.d. random variables such that $X_n = \Sigma_{t=1}^{n}U\_{t}$. A pdf (i.e. f.d.d.) of such process $X$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is then given by $f\_{X}(x) = f\_{X\_1}(x\_{1})f\_{X\_2-X\_1}(x\_{2}-x\_{1}) \dots f\_{X\_n - X\_{n-1}}(x\_{n}-x\_{n-1})$ if a dist. of $X\_{t}$ on $\mathbb{R}$ is known for all $t \in T$. 


## III
---
A simple [random walk]() on $\mathbb{Z}^{\geq}$, introduced by Karl Pearson in 1905, is an elementary example of a stochastic process. Formally, a partial sum process $(S\_{t})\_{t \in T}$ consists of $S\_0 = 0$ and $S\_t = \Sigma\_{k=1}^{t}X\_k$ with uniformly distributed ind. random variables $X\_{k} \in \lbrace +1, -1 \rbrace$ is the simple random walk **(#4)** with $\operatorname{E}S\_t = 0$ and $\operatorname{Var}S\_{t} = t$. Note that $(I\_{t})\_{t \in T}$ consists of $I\_{t} = (X\_{t} + 1)/2$ is a sequence of Bernoulli trials with parameter $p$, and hence $R\_{t} = \Sigma\_{k=1}^{t} I\_{k}$ follows $\operatorname{Bin}(t,p)$. If $X\_{t} = 1$ with $p$ and $X\_{t} = -1$ with $q=1-p$, then $\operatorname{E}X\_{t} = 2p-1$ and $\operatorname{Var}X\_{t} = 4pq$, and therefore, a random variable $Y\_{t} = 2R\_{t}-t$ has $\operatorname{E}Y\_{t} = t(2p-1)$ and $\operatorname{Var}Y\_{t} = 4tpq$ both determined by $p$ **(#5)**.

The [Poisson (counting) process]() is another second order process with stationary and ind. increment alongside the simple random walk. Since the process consists of i.i.d. $X_t \sim \operatorname{Exp}(\lambda)$, it has $\operatorname{E}X\_{t} = \operatorname{Var}X\_{t} = \lambda{t}$ for every $t \in [0,\infty)$, and the sample paths are monotonically increasing; Remark. given a set of complex-valued random variable $X\_t \in L^2(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ such that $\operatorname{E}X\_t = 0$, if the inner product in the $L^2$-space is properly given by $\langle X\_{t\_1}, X_{t\_2} \rangle = \operatorname{E}[X\_{t_1}\overline{X}\_{t\_2}]$ (i.e. ${\left\lVert X\_t \right\rVert}\_{2} = \sqrt{\langle X\_t, X\_t \rangle}$), then a set $(X\_t)_{t \in T}$ forms a Hilbert space $H$. Therefore, if the index set $T$ contains non-negative real numbers, then $(X\_t)\_{t \in T}$ can be regarded as a curve $C$ exsiting in $H$;

Now suppose $X$ and $Y$ be dep. and ind. random variables, respectively. An OLS estimation can be conducted in [time series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_series) literature if the stochastic processes (i.e. panel data) hold the assumptions of linear regression (taken for cross sectional data) whilst being stationary **(#6)**. Non-stationarity usually refers to trend, seasonality, and heteroskedasticity. We can brutely execute the [log-differencing]() to stablise the mean of a time series, and then compute autocorrelations along with the [acf/pacf plots](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DeORzP0go5I) to stablise the variacne. Or, we use the [STL decomposition](https://otexts.com/fpp2/stl.html) $y\_{t} = S\_{t} + T\_{t} + R\_{t}$ or $\log{y\_{t}} = \log{S\_{t}} + \log{T\_{t}} + \log{R\_{t}}$, where $S\_{t}$, $T\_{t}$, $R\_{t}$ are the seasonal, trend, and remainder parts, respectively.


## IV
---
To be more technical, let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ be a probability space, $X = (X\_t)\_{t \in T}$ be a stochastic process, and $F = (\mathcal{F}\_t)\_{t \in T}$ be a [filtration]() which represents a family of sub-$\sigma$-algebras $\mathcal{F}\_{0} \subseteq \mathcal{F}\_1 \subseteq \dots \subseteq \mathcal{F}\_{s} \subseteq \mathcal{F}\_{t} \subseteq \dots \subseteq \mathcal{F}$. For all $t \in T$, we say $X$ is [adapted]() to the filtration $F$ if $X\_{t}:\Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ is $\mathcal{F}\_{t}$-measurable, and [predictable]() by $F$ if $X\_{t+1}$ is $\mathcal{F}\_{t}$-measurable. In particular, the [natural filtration]() $\mathcal{F}\_{t} = \sigma(X\_{s} \,\vert\, s \leq t)$ is mostly used as the smallest filtration containing all information up to time $t$. Furthermore, suppose two filtrations $F$ and $G$ are on any fixed sample space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$, then we say $F$ is coarser than $G$ and $G$ is finer than $F$, denoted by $F \preceq G$, if $F\_t \subseteq G\_t$ for all $t \in T$.

A random variable $\tau: \Omega \to T$ on a [filtered probability space]() $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, F, P)$ is called a [stopping time]() with respect to $F$ if $\lbrace \tau \leq t \rbrace \in \mathcal{F}\_t$ for all $t \in T$, and all information stacked up to $\tau$ is encoded by a [stopped $\sigma$-algebra]() $\mathcal{F}\_{\tau} = \lbrace A \in \mathcal{F}\_{\infty}: A \cap \lbrace \tau \leq t \rbrace \in \mathcal{F}\_t, \,\forall t \in T \rbrace$, where an event $A \in S$. Trivially, $\tau$ is a stopping time if and only if $\lbrace \tau > t \rbrace \in \mathcal{F}\_t$ for all $t \in T$. If an arbitrary $\tau \in T$ is a stopping time with respect to $F$, and $F$ is coarser than $G$, then $\tau$ is a stopping time with respect to $G$. Moreover, if $\tau\_1, \tau\_2 \in F$ are stopping times with respect to $F$, then (i) $\tau\_1 \vee \tau\_2 = \max \lbrace \tau\_1, \tau\_2 \rbrace$; (ii) $\tau\_1 \wedge \tau\_2 = \min \lbrace \tau\_1, \tau\_2 \rbrace$; (iii) $\tau\_1 + \tau\_2$; are all a stopping time with respect to $F$.

Loosely speaking, $\tau$ decides whether or not to stop evolving $X$ at time $t$ based only on information up to $t$ (i.e. no future information). That is, if $\mathcal{F}\_t = F$ for all $t \in T$, then every $t$ are a stopping time. Similarly, if $\mathcal{F}\_t = \lbrace \Omega, \emptyset \rbrace$ for all $t \in T$, then $\tau(\omega) = t$ for all $\omega \in \Omega$ with some $t \in T\_{\infty}$ **(#8)**. Whilst the examples contain impracticality, a [first hitting time]() $\tau\_B = \inf \lbrace t: X\_t \in B \rbrace$ is a useful stopping time which $\lbrace \tau\_B = t \rbrace = \lbrace X\_0 \notin B, \dots, X\_s \notin B, X\_t \in B \rbrace = \bigcap\_{r=0}^{s} {\lbrace X\_r \in B \rbrace}^c \cap \lbrace X\_t \in B \rbrace \in \mathcal{F}\_{t}$. In contrast, $\tau\_{B}^{\prime} = \sup \lbrace t: X\_t \in B \rbrace$ is not a stopping time since a full trajectory of $X$ throughout $T$ must be given in order to have a stopping strategy.
