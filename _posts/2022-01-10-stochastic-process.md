## I
---
Suppose $T$ is a [totally ordered set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_order) which denotes time, $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is a probability space, and $(\mathbb{S}, \mathcal{S})$ is a state space. A [stochastic process]() $X = (X\_{t})\_{t \in T}$ is a sequence of random variables $X_{t}: \Omega \to \mathbb{S}$ which may or may not be well-defined at each $t$. We say $X$ is discrete if $T = \mathbb{N} \cup \lbrace \infty \rbrace$, or continuous if $T = [0,\infty)$, and often take $\mathbb{S} \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ **(#1)**. A process $X$ is a function of $\omega \in \Omega$ and $t \in T$, denoted by $X:T\times\Omega\to\mathbb{S}$, and thus notations such as $X(t)$, $X\_{t}(\omega)$, or $X(t,\omega)$ are interchangibly used for $X\_{t}$. A function $X(\,\cdot, \omega): T \to \mathbb{S}$ with a fixed $\omega \in \Omega$ is called a [sample path]() or realisation of $X$, and a $\sigma$-algebra generated by $\mathbb{S}$ over $T$ is a space full of pdfs and/or pmfs.

Information relevant to $X$ within a finite time interval $T = \lbrace 1, 2, \dots, \rbrace$ will conveyed by the $\sigma$-algebra $\sigma(X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n)$ generated by the sets $\bigcap_{k=1}^{n}\lbrace \omega: X_k(\omega) \leq x_k \rbrace$, where $x_k \in \mathbb{R}$. Thus we can access the full information, i.e. $X$ is running in the infinite time, from the $\sigma$-algebra generated by the union of sets $\bigcup_{k=1}^{n}X_k$. Particularly, for any $x = (x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the joint cdf of a process is calculated by $F_X(x) = \mathbb{P}((-\infty, x])$ and a collection of $F_X(\cdot)$ is called a [finite-dimensional distribution]() (f.d.d.) of $X$. Namely, f.d.d. is the dist. of a $\mathbb{R}^n$-valued random vector $X = (X_{t_1}, X_{t_2}, \dots, X_{t_n})$ in which we can only collect finite amount of information via experiments.

A f.d.d. however does not correspond to a product measure unless the selected timestamps yield independent random variables. That is, $\mathbb{P}((-\infty, x]) = P(\lbrace \omega: (X_{t_1}(\omega) \leq x_1, X_{t_2}(\omega) \leq x_2, \dots, X_{t_n}(\omega)\leq x_n)\rbrace$. To investigate if the f.d.d. of $X$ suffices to uniquely determine $X$, we can calculate the 1st moment $\mu_X(t) = \operatorname{E}X_t$, and the 2nd central moment $\gamma_{XX}(t_1,t_2) = \operatorname{Cov}(X_{t_1}, X_{t_2})$, where $t_{1},t_{2} \in T$. Normalising the latter that we call the [autocovariance]() yields the [autocorrelation]() $\rho_{XX}(t_1, t_2) = \gamma_{XX}(t_1, t_2) / \sigma_{X}(t_1)\sigma_{X}(t_2)$, and we can further generalise the definition $\rho_{XX}(t, t+\tau) = \gamma_{XX}(t, t+\tau) / \sigma_{X}(t)\sigma_{X}(t+\tau)$, where $\tau$ denotes a time lag.


## II
---
For every $s, t \in T$ with $s \leq t$, a stochastic process $X$ has [independent increments]() (i.e. differences) if $X\_t - X\_s$ is independent of $\mathcal{F}\_s$, and [stationary increments]() if $F\_{X\_t - X\_s} = F\_{X\_{t-s}}$. In particular, if $T = \mathbb{N} \cup \lbrace\infty\rbrace$, then $X$ has stationary and ind. increments if and only if $X$ is a partial sum process associated with a sequence of i.i.d. random variables $U$ such that $X_t = \Sigma_{k=1}^{t}U_k$. Meanwhile the statistical properties of $X_t$ at any fixed $t$ shall be determined by its marginal dist. (as usual), but also its joint dist. because $X_t$ are likely to be relavant to some neighbourhoods marignals. In fact, the relationship with neighbourhoods can change as $t$ moves, results a catastrophe for the investigation.

For all $n \in \mathbb{N}$, if the joint cdf is given by $F_{(X_{t_1}, X_{t_1}, \cdots, X_{t_n})}(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n) = F_{(X_{t_{1+\tau}}, X_{t_{2+\tau}}, \cdots, X_{t_{n+\tau}})}(x_{1+\tau}, x_{2+\tau}, \dots, x_{n+\tau})$, i.e. the f.d.d. of $X$ is invariant under the time translation, then $X$ is [strict-sense stationary]() (sss). If we were given $n \in \lbrace 1, 2, \cdots, N \rbrace$, then $X$ is [$N$-th order stationary]() **(#2)**. Instead, if $\mu_X(t) = \mu_X$ and $\gamma_{XX}(t_1, t_2) = \gamma_{XX}(\tau, 0)$ with $\tau = t_2 - t_1$, then $X$ is [weak-sense stationary]() (wss). We resort to a weaker condition because the ss-stationarity is highly restrictive. //

If $X$ is stationary, we have the [time average]() $\bar{x} = {1\over{2T}}\int_{-T}^{T} X(t)\, \mathrm{d}t$ and the [ensemble average]() $\mu\_{X} = \operatorname{E}X(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} zf\_{X\_t}(z)\,\mathrm{d}z$. If it is true that $\lim\_{T\to\infty}\bar{x} = \mu\_X$, then such a process is [mean-ergodic](). By virtue of ergodicity, we may evaluate the statistical properties of a process by using a single long sample path, whilst observing all possible samples at some fixed time $t$ is impractical. Similarly, given the time average estimate $\bar{\gamma}\_{XX}(\tau) = {1\over{2T}}\int\_{-T}^{T}\,(X(t+\tau)-\mu_X)(X(t)-\mu_X) \, \mathrm{d}t$, if it is true that $\lim\_{T\to\infty}\bar{\gamma}\_{XX}(\tau) = \gamma\_{XX}(\tau)$, then such a process [autocovariance-ergodic](). A process that is both mean and autocovariance-ergodic may be called wide-sense ergodic **(#3)**.

---
A simple [random walk]() on $\mathbb{Z}$, introduced by Karl Pearson in 1905, is an elementary example of a stochastic process. Specifically, a partial sum process $(S\_{t})\_{t \in T}$ consists of $S\_0 = 0$ and $S\_t = \Sigma\_{k=1}^{t}X\_k$ with uniformly distributed ind. random variables $X\_{k} \in \lbrace +1, -1 \rbrace$ is the simple random walk **(#4)** with $\operatorname{E}S\_t = 0$ and $\operatorname{Var}S\_{t} = t$. Note that $(I\_{t})\_{t \in T}$ consists of $I\_{t} = (X\_{t} + 1)/2$ is a sequence of Bernoulli trials with parameter $p$, and hence $R\_{t} = \Sigma\_{k=1}^{t} I\_{k}$ follows $\operatorname{Bin}(t,p)$. If $X\_{t} = 1$ with $p$ and $X\_{t} = -1$ with $q=1-p$, then $\operatorname{E}X\_{t} = 2p-1$ and $\operatorname{Var}X\_{t} = 4pq$, and therefore, a random variable $Y\_{t} = 2R\_{t}-t$ has $\operatorname{E}Y\_{t} = t(2p-1)$ and $\operatorname{Var}Y\_{t} = 4tpq$ both determined by $p$ **(#5)**.

The [Poisson (counting) process]() is another second order process with stationary and ind. increment alongside the simple random walk.
