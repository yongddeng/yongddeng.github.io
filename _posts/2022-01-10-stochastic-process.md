## I
---
Suppose $T$ is a totally ordered set referring discrete or continuous time, $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is a probability space, and $(\mathbb{S}, \mathcal{S})$ is a state space. A [stochastic process]() $X = (X_t)_{t \in T}$ refers types of sequence of random variables, where each $X_t: \Omega \to \mathbb{S}$ is not necessarily well-defined. In general, we say $X$ is discrete when $T = \mathbb{N} \cup \{\infty\}$ and continuous when $T = [0,\infty)$, and also regard $(\mathbb{S}, \mathcal{S})$ as $(\mathbb{R}^n, \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}^n})$ with usual Borel $\sigma$-algebras $\mathcal{B}$. In fact, the process $X$ is a function of $\omega \in \Omega$ and $t \in T$ in which we denote by $X:T\times\Omega\to\mathbb{S}$, and various notations such as $X(t)$, $X_t(\omega)$, or $X(t,\omega)$ are interchangibly used for $X_t$. With any fixed $\omega \in \Omega$, we say a function $X(\,\cdot, \omega): T \to \mathbb{S}$ is a [sample path]() or a realisation of the  process **(#1)**.

Every relevant information about such a process during a finite time interval $T = \{1, 2, \dots, n\}$ is conveyed by the $\sigma$-algebra $\sigma(X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n)$ that is generated by the sets $\bigcap_{k=1}^{n}\{\omega: X_k(\omega) \leq x_k\}$, where $x_k \in \mathbb{R}$. We similarly access the full information of a process running in the infinite time from the $\sigma$-algebra generated by the union of sets $\bigcup_{i=1}^{n}X_k$. Then, for any $x = (x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n$, we calculate a joint cdf of a process $F_X(x) = \mathbb{P}((-\infty, x])$, and call a collection of $F_X(\cdot)$ a [finite-dimensional distribution]() (f.d.d.) of $X$. In other words, f.d.d. is just the jargon for the distribution of a $\mathbb{R}^n$-valued random vector $X = (X_{t_1}, X_{t_2}, \dots, X_{t_n})$, where we can only gather finite amount of information from experiments.

Note that, a f.d.d. does not correspond to a product measure unless the selected timestamps yield independent random variables, thus we usually calculate $\mathbb{P}((-\infty, x]) = P(\{\omega: (X_{t_1}(\omega) \leq x_1, X_{t_2}(\omega) \leq x_2, \dots, X_{t_n}(\omega)\leq x_n)\}$, whilst wondering if the f.d.d. of $X$ suffices to determine the uniqueness of $X$. For investigations, we calculate the 1st moment $\mu_X(t) = \operatorname{E}X_t$, and the 2nd central moment $\gamma_{XX}(t_1,t_2) = \operatorname{Cov}(X_{t_1}, X_{t_2}) = \operatorname{E}[X_{t_1} - \mu_{X}(t_1)][X_{t_2} - \mu_{X}(t_2)]$, for some $t_{1},t_{2} \in T$. Moreover, we normalise the [autocovariance]() to obtain the [autocorrelation]() $\rho_{XX}(t_1, t_2) = \gamma_{XX}(t_1, t_2) / \sigma_{X}(t_1)\sigma_{X}(t_2)$, and may generalise the notation by $\rho_{XX}(t, t+\tau) = \gamma_{XX}(t, t+\tau) / \sigma_{X}(t)\sigma_{X}(t+\tau)$, where $\tau$ denotes a time lag.


## II
---
In terms of Hilber space, given a set of complex-valued $X_t \in L^2(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ whose $\operatorname{E}X_t = 0$ and $\operatorname{E}|X|^2 < \infty$, if the inner product (and so the norm) in the $L^2$-space can be defined by $\langle X_{t_1}, X_{t_2} \rangle = \operatorname{E}X_{t_1}\overline{X}_{t_2}$ (and $||X_t||_2 = \sqrt{\langle X_t, X_t \rangle}$), then a set of $X_t$ form a Hilbert space $H$. In particular, if a set of $(X_t)_{t \in T}$ is indexed by a set of nonnegative real numbers, then $(X_t)_{t \in T}$ can be regarded as a curve $C$ exsiting in $H$ (add orthogornality).//. 프로세스에서 특정 time t의 $X_t$가 어떤 특징을 가지는지 알기 위해서는 t에서의 marginal dist. 뿐만 아니라 t근처 과거의 marginal dists들과 joint dist.를 봐야 할 것 이다. 다만 관계가 시간에 따라 변할 수 있기 때문에, 프로세스가 stationarity한지 아래와 같이 배워보자.

For all $n \in \mathbb{N}$ and $t_1, t_2, \dots, t_n, \dots, t_{n + \tau} \in T$, if the joint cdf $F_{(X_{t_1}, X_{t_1}, \cdots, X_{t_n})}(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n) = F_{(X_{t_{1+\tau}}, X_{t_{2+\tau}}, \cdots, X_{t_{n+\tau}})}(x_{1+\tau}, x_{2+\tau}, \dots, x_{n+\tau})$, namely, if the f.d.d. of $X$ is invariant under time translation, then $X$ is [strict-sense stationary]() (sss). If we were given $n \in \{1, 2, \cdots, N\}$, then $X$ is simply  said to be [$N$-th order stationary]() **(#2)**. Nevertheless, we resort to a weaker condition as these definitions are highly restrictive. That is, for all $t, t_1, t_2 \in T$, if $\mu_X(t) = \mu_X$ and $\gamma_{XX}(t_1, t_2) = \gamma_{XX}(\tau, 0)$ with $\tau = t_2 - t_1$, then $X$ is [weak-sense stationary]() (wss). // Having ind. increments is one example of a property that is determined by the f.f.d., so ____. Then, the f.f.d. of any process is consistent, that is, $\lim_{}$. ____.

Furthermore, for a stationary process $X$, we can define both the [time average]() $\bar{x} = {1\over{2T}}\int_{-T}^{T} X(t)\, \mathrm{d}t$ and the [ensemble average]() $\mu_X = \operatorname{E}X(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} zf_{X_t}(z)\,\mathrm{d}z$. Subsequently, if it is true that $\lim_{T\to\infty}\bar{x} = \mu_X$, then such a process is [mean-ergodic](). By virtue of ergodicity, we may evaluate the statistical properties of a process by using a single long sample path, whilst observing all possible samples at some fixed time $t$ is impractical. Similarly, given the time average estimate $\bar{\gamma}_{XX}(\tau) = {1\over{2T}}\int_{-T}^{T}\,(X(t+\tau)-\mu_X)\cdot(X(t)-\mu_X) \, \mathrm{d}t$, if it is true that $\lim_{T\to\infty}\bar{\gamma}_{XX}(\tau) = \gamma_{XX}(\tau)$, then such a process [autocovariance-ergodic](). A process that is both mean and autocovariance-ergodic may be called wide-sense ergodic **(#3)**.
