# Random Variable
---
A random variable $X$ maps an event onto a measurable space where the dist. function lies on. Drawing a number of samples $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n$ will in fact accumulate elements of randomness because each value of sample is indeterminate until an excution of experiment. We decided to use the letter $X$ and distinguish itself from an ordinary algebraic variable $x$ that is often assigned to a constant number.


## I
---
Given two sample spaces $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ and $(\mathbb{S}, \mathcal{S})$, a map $X: \Omega \to \mathbb{S}$ is called an $\mathbb{S}$-valued random variable, if $X^{-1}(A) = \{X \in A\} = \{\omega: X(\omega) \in A\} \in \mathcal{F}$ for all $A \subset \mathbb{S}$, and a [random variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_variable) is specifically an $\mathbb{R}$-valued $X$. We consider $X$ is discrete if $P(X \in A) = 1$ for a finite or countable $A \subset \mathbb{R}$, or continuous if $P(X = x) = 0$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$. By constuction, we can define the [distribution function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumulative_distribution_function) (cdf) $F_{X}(x) = \mathbb{P}((-\infty, x]) = P(X \leq x)$ for any random variable $X$, where $\mathbb{P}$ is an [induced probability measure](https://stats.stackexchange.com/questions/451503/what-is-an-induced-probability-function) **(#1)**. If $X$ is discrete, then $F_{X}(x) = \Sigma_{x_j \leq x} p_X(x_j)$, where $p_X(x_j) = P(X = x_j)$ is the [probability function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_mass_function) (pmf). If $X$ is continuous, then $F_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f_X(t)\, dt$, where $f_X = {d \over dx}F_X$ is the [density function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_density_function) (pdf).

Suppose $X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ and $g: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ are a random variable and a Borel measurable function, respectively, then a composition $g \circ X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ is also a random variable. Subsequently, we adapt the [change of variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_of_variables) whereby only concerning $X$ suffices to calculate the probabilitiy of a new event $P(Y \in B) = P(X \in g^{-1}(B))$ for all $B \subset \mathbb{R}$, where $g^{-1}(B) = \lbrace \omega: g(X(\omega)) \in B \rbrace \in \mathcal{B}\_\mathbb{R}$. In particular, if $X$ is discrete, then $Y$ is also discrete, and thus $p\_Y(y) = \Sigma\_{\lbrace x\,:\, g(x)=y \rbrace} p\_X(x)$. Note that $p\_Y(y) = p\_X(x)$ if and only if $g$ is injective. On the other hand, if $X$ is continuous, then $Y$ can be discrete, continuous, or neither. A number of combination yields a more general notation for $p_Y(y) = f_Y(y) = \int\_{\lbrace x\,:\, g(x) = y \rbrace} f\_X(x)\, \mathrm{d}x$.

In $n$-dimensional cases, we use a [random vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivariate_random_variable) $\boldsymbol{X}: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}^n$ as a multivariate function, which then defines a [joint distribution function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joint_probability_distribution) $F_{\boldsymbol{X}}(\boldsymbol{x}) = P(\boldsymbol{X} \leq \boldsymbol{x})$, where $\boldsymbol{x} = (x_1, \dots, x_n)$ and $\{\boldsymbol{X} \leq \boldsymbol{x}\} = \bigcap_{k=1}^{n}\{X_k \leq x_k\} = \{X_1 \leq x_1, \dots, X_n \leq x_n\}$ **(#2)**. In fact, the distribution of $X_1$ (i.e. low-dim: 1), that is as an element of $\boldsymbol{X} = (X_1, X_2)$ (i.e. high-dim: 2), can be obtained by summing up the joint probabilities of the variables other than $X_1$. Whilst both $F_{X_1}(x_1) = P(X_1 \leq x_1, X_2 < \infty)$ and $F_{X_2}(x_2) = P(X_1 < \infty, X_2 \leq x_2)$ are called [marginal distribution functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marginal_distribution) of $\boldsymbol{X}$, we begin to ask with excitement whether knowing about a single event occurring has significant effects on knowing about other events occuring.


## II
---
A sequence $(A_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ of events is [independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_(probability_theory)) (ind.), if $P(\,\bigcap_{k=1}^{n} A_{k}) = \prod_{k=1}^{n} P(A_{k})$ **(#3)**. Thus a sequence $(X_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ of random variables is ind., if $P(\,\bigcap_{k=1}^{n}\{X_k \in B_k\}) = \prod_{k=1}^{n} P(X_k \in B_k)$, where $B_k \subset \mathbb{R}$ is Borel measurable. Under the assumption of independence, the joint cdf $F_{\boldsymbol{X}}(\boldsymbol{x})$ is equal to the product of the marginal cdfs $\prod_{k=1}^{n} F_{X_k}(x_k)$ for any $\boldsymbol{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$. If, in particular, both $X$ and $Y$ permit pdfs., then we use the [Fubini's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%27s_theorem) to calculate $F_{X,Y}(x,y) = F_X(x)F_Y(y) \coloneqq \int_{-\infty}^{(x,y)} f_{X,Y}(t,s)\, (dt \times ds) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f_X(t)\, dt \int_{-\infty}^{y} f_Y(s)\, ds$, or, equivalently, $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = f_X(x)f_Y(y)$. In similar fashion, if two ind. random variables $X$ and $Y$ permit pmfs, then we can have $p_{X,Y}(x,y) = p_X(x)p_Y(y)$. 

However, if two events $A$ and $B$ are dependent (i.e. jointly distributed), then we need to calculate the [conditional probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conditional_probability) $P(B \vert A) = P(A\cap{B}) / P(A)$, where $P(A \cap B) = P(A \vert B)P(B) = P(B \vert A)P(A)$ due to the [multiplication law](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/boundless-statistics/chapter/probability-rules). Notice that $P(A) > 0$ rescales the value to be at most $1$. Whereas, the reverse conditional probability $P(A \vert B) = P(B \vert A)P(A)/P(B)$ is due to the [Bayes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bayes%27_theorem), and the conditioning parts are reduced to $P(A \vert B) = P(A)$ and $P(B \vert A) = P(B)$ whenever $A$ and $B$ are ind.. In particular, if the underlying sample space $\Omega$ is discrete, and $(A_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ are the [partitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_of_a_set) of $\Omega$ **(#4)**, the [law of total probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_total_probability) yields the marginal $P(B) = \Sigma_{k=1}^{n}P(A_k \cap B)= \Sigma_{k=1}^{n}P(B \vert A_k)P(A_k)$.

For instance, if two discrete $X$ and $Y$ are dependent, then the [conditional cdf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conditional_probability_distribution) at some $X = x$ is given by $F_{Y \vert X=x}(y) = \Sigma_{y_j\leq{y}}p_{Y \vert X=x}(y_j)$, where $p_{Y \vert X=x}(y) = p_{X,Y}(x,y)/p_X(x)$ is the [conditional pmf](https://www.statlect.com/glossary/conditional-probability-mass-function) of $Y$ given $X=x$. It follows that the marginal pmf $p_Y(y) = \int_{x} p_{Y \vert X=x}(y)p_X(x)\, dx$ is an expected value. Similarly, if two continuous $X$ and $Y$ are dependent, then we calculate $F_{Y \vert X=x}(y) = \int_{-\infty}^{y} f_{Y \vert X=x}(y_j)\, dy_j$, where $f_{Y \vert X=x}(y) = f_{X,Y}(x,y)/f_X(x)$ is the [conditional pdf](https://www.statlect.com/glossary/conditional-probability-density-function) of $Y$ given $X \in (x-\varepsilon, x+\varepsilon)$. Otherwise, a random vector directly produces marginals, i.e.some  $\boldsymbol{Z} = (X, Y)$ with the [joint pmf](https://www.statlect.com/glossary/joint-probability-mass-function#:~:text=The%20joint%20probability%20mass%20function,be%20equal%20to%20that%20point.) $p_{X,Y}(x,y)$ produces the [marginal pmf](https://www.statlect.com/glossary/marginal-probability-mass-function#:~:text=The%20marginal%20probability%20mass%20function,belong%20to%20the%20support%20and) for $X$ and $Y$ by $p_X(x) = \Sigma_{y} p_{X,Y}(x,y)$ and $p_Y(y) = \Sigma_{x}p(x,y)$, respectively. 


## III
---
We now study more about the zero-one laws. Suppose $(\mathcal{F}_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ is a sequence of sub-$\sigma$-algebras $\mathcal{F}_{n} = \sigma(A_k: k \leq n) = \sigma(\bigcup_{k=1}^{n}A_k)$. If we let $\mathcal{G}_n = \sigma(A_{k}: k \geq n) = \sigma(\bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty}A_k)$, then the set $\mathcal{G} = \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} \mathcal{G}_n$ is the [tail-$\sigma$-algebra](https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/3184787/what-is-the-tail-sigma-field) of $(\mathcal{F}_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$. By construction, $\mathcal{F}_n \subseteq \mathcal{F}_{n+1}$ and $\mathcal{G} \subset \mathcal{F} = \sigma(\bigcup_{k \geq 1}\mathcal{F}_k) = \sigma(A_k: k \geq 1)$, and as each $\mathcal{G}_n$ contains information beyond time $n$, the set $\mathcal{G}$ contains information beyond time $n$ for any $n \in \mathbb{N}$. Subsequently, given $(\mathcal{F_n})_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ consists of ind. $\mathcal{F}_n$, the [Kolmogorov zero-one law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kolmogorov%27s_zero%E2%80%93one_law) states that the probability of any [tail event](https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/1047306/events-in-the-tail-sigma-algebra) $A \in \mathcal{G}$ is either $0$ or $1$ a.s.. In other words, the occurence of tail events can still be determined even though a finite number of $\mathcal{F}_n$ being at the front of a row are effaced.

The same behaviour is preserved in $\mathbb{R}$. If we let $B \in \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}\mathcal{S}_n$ be the tail event, where $\mathcal{S}_n = \sigma(X_n, X_{n+1}, \dots) = \sigma(\bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty}X_k)$ is independent to each other, then $B$ belongs to every one of the $\sigma$-algebras $\{\mathcal{S}_n, \mathcal{S}_{n+1}, \dots \}$, and thus we can interpret $B$ independently from any finite subsets of $\{X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n\}$. Abstractly, we let $\mathcal{F} = \sigma(X_1, X_2, \dots X_n, X_{n+1}, \dots$) and $\mathcal{S} = \sigma(X_n, X_{n+1}, \dots)$, and suppose $B \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\mathcal{S} \subset \mathcal{F}$, and so omitting $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_{n-1}$ does not affect on whether $B$ is contained in $\mathcal{G}$. Practically, the Komolgorov zero-one law implies that the limit exists either with probability 0 or 1 (depending on the dist. of $X_n$), and if it dose, there exists a constant $c$ such that $\lim_{n\to\infty}X_n = c$ with probability 1.

For example, the event that the sequence converges **(#5)**, and the event that its sum converges are both tail events. Notice that for any $N \in \mathbb{N}$, the tail-$\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{G}$ can be generated by $\limsup_{n+N\to\infty}X_{n+N}$, and so $\limsup_{n\to\infty}X_n \in \mathcal{G}$ is a tail event. We recall the Borel-Cantelli lemmas which states that $P(\limsup_{n\to\infty}X_n) \in \{0,1\}$. Whilst the 1st lemma states that the probability of unions of the tail segments is at most the sum of the probability of each segments, the independence assumption in the 2nd lemma makes the tail $\sigma$-algebras trivial **(#6)**, thus able to tell that the event occurs a.s. (under a sufficient condition) due to the Komolgorov zero-one law (which dose not really clarifies whether $0$ or $1$ is correct).
