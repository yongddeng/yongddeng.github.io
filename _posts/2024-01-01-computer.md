## I
---
The [analytical engine]() (1837), designed by English mathematician Charles Babbage, is regarded as the first conceptualised general-purpose digital computer. It featured an arithmetic logic unit, control flow with conditional branching and loops, and memory modules. The [Turing machine]() (1936), proposed by Alan Turing, established a theoretical framework for analysis of computational capability and limitation, laying the foundation for Computer Science. The [electronic numerical integrator and computer]() (ENIAC) (1945), developed, by John Mauchly and J. Presper Eckert, became the first programmable, electronic, and general-purpose digital computer.

Computers vary in size, processing power, and their purposes, but often fall into one of the followings: i) [personal computers]() (PCs): extremely versatile and typically used by individuals for a wide range of office works, web browsing, gaming, multimedia, emulation, programming, and etc; ii) [mainframes]():  designed for high-volume transaction processing, bulk data handling, and mission-critical system that demand exceptional reliability and security; iii) [supercomputers](): deliver extraordinary processing speed and computational power, ideal for intensive tasks such as climate modeling, simulations, and intricate computations required in physics or cryptography.

The [motherboard]() serves the physical circuitries and connections for three key components of [Von Neumann architecture]() (1945). This is the hub where the CPU socket, RAM slots, storage interfaces, I/O ports, and other peripherals communicate via the [buses]() and [chipsets](). The module in recent computers have expanded slots for GPUs, sound cards, or extra storages, intended to offer more capabilities or flexibilities for future upgrades. The BIOS/UEFI [firmware]() embedded into the module performs [self-tests]() (POST) when the power supplies, then the bootloader (e.g. Linux's GRUB, Windows's Boot Manager) loads the kernel from the SSD into RAM.

## II
---
### **1. Central Processing Unit**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

The [central processing unit]() (CPU, or processor) executes a set of instructions, called a [program](), through the [fetch-decode-execute]() cycle. Each core of a CPU has its own [arithmetic logic unit]() (ALU) and [control unit]() (CU). Clock speed (in GHz) affects execution speed, and multiple cores enable parallel processing. Modern CPUs have 4 to 64 cores with [floating point unit]() (FPU). In terms of memory modules, [registers](), the fastest memory, temporarily store data and instructions that are immediately needed by the processor. The [cache]() hierarchy ('L1': smallest-fastest, 'L2': larger-slower, 'L3': largest-shared across cores) can further reduce memory latency.

### **2. Random Access Memory**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

A computer memory is an array of bytes, each with a unique address. [Random access memory]() (RAM), register, and cache are therefore distinct abstractions. [Dynamic RAM]() (DRAM) latency depends on data access patterns and the physical design of memory cells. Access is fastest when data is in an active row (i.e. contiguous), but latency grows by $T_{\text{precharge}} + T_{\text{row activation}} + T_{\text{col. access}}$ when a new row is required. [Static RAM]() (SRAM) is known for its high speed and stability despite its higher cost, and therefore appropriate for a cache memory. Notably, persistent storage can be used as [virtual memory]() when a process exceeds the RAM limits.

<img src="https://www.refsmmat.com/courses/751/images/computer-arch.svg" width="500" alt="https://www.refsmmat.com/courses/751/notes/index.html">

### **3. Persistent Storage**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

Persistent storage includes [hard disk drives]() (HDDs) and [solid-state drives]() (SSDs), both non-volatile and capable of retaining data without power. HDDs are mechanical devices which have slower access speeds due to the physical movement required to read data. In contrast, SSDs use electronic signals for much faster access. In particular, many computing devices released since 2016 feature [non-volatile memory express]() (NVMe) SSDs, which operate over the [peripheral component interconnect express]() (PCIe) to enable direct communication between the SSD and CPU. Flash memory, similar to SSDs, is also used for storing data and applications.

## III
---
### **<a name="gpu"></a>4. Graphics Processing Unit**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

The [graphics processing unit]() (GPU), an integral of a graphics card, operates a [single instruction, multiple threads]() (SIMT) execution of relatively simple and repetitive tasks. Compared to CPUs, GPUs have significantly more cores, with each operatating at a lower clock speed, yet they ensure efficient computation and high throughput when paired with high-bandwidth memory. For performance metrics, [floating point operations per second]() (FLOPS) measures the GPU’s computational capacity, while [floating point operations]() (FLOPs) indicates the computation demands of NNs which, in the case of LLMs, are influenced by the number of parameters and tokens.

The [data processing unit]() (DPU), a relatively newer programmable processor that is typically based on the [arm architecture](), aims to optimise data-centric tasks especially in large data centers, such as data movement, storage (e.g. compression and encryption), and network packet processing (e.g. routing and security), rather than compute-focused tasks. DPUs offload data management from CPUs and orchestrate data flow between CPUs, GPUs, and storages. NVIDIA introduced DPUs with robust security features such as isolation of data flows with hardware-level encryption and access control, indeed critical for cloud-native and virtualised environments.

#### **4.2. //**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

The [streaming multiprocessor]() (SM) is a core processing unit responsible for executing thread blocks. A [thread]() is the smallest unit of execution that handles a portion of tensor operation (e.g. an element-wise multiplication), [warps]() are threads organised with uniform size to execute instructions under the SIMT model, and a [thread block]() is a collection of warps assigned to each SM. A more thread blocks than the number of SMs in modern GPUs should be dispatched to prevent underutilisation of GPU. When a core stalls, the [warp scheduler]() facilitates the switching between warps within the SMs, allowing for effective latency masking and higher throughput.

<img src="https://media.springernature.com/lw685/springer-static/image/chp%3A10.1007%2F978-981-15-6401-7_66-1/MediaObjects/491764_0_En_66-1_Fig9_HTML.jpg" width="500" alt="https://blog.paperspace.com/a-complete-anatomy-of-a-graphics-card-case-study-of-the-nvidia-a100/">

The [cuda core]() in a SM is a general-purpose processing units, essentially an ALU, designed for parallel computing across thousands of threads. Each core behaves like ALU, performing basic arithmetic ($+$, $\times$) and logical operations ($\land$, $\lor$, $\neg$). A single cuda core typically handles one instruction per cycle per thread, supporting both FP and INT operations, making it well-suited for tasks including graphics rendering, simulations, and general-purpose parallel workloads. However, cuda cores lack abilities in handling specialised computation such as matrix mutiplications, while NVIDIA architectures include additional hardware to address these limitations. 

The [tensor core](), located near cuda cores, was first introduced in the Volta architecture (V100) to perform [fused multiply-add]() (FMA) operations (i.e. 64 MMA) in [mixed precision](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=b5dAmcBKxHg) per GPU clock cycle. Each core runs $4 \times 4$ matrix multiplications within a single cycle, thus the A100 with 432 tensor cores achieves a peak TF32 performance of 156 TFLOPS. Mixed precision allows input representation of FP16 (2 bytes/element), half the precision of the outputs. It improves NNs' computation efficiency in training and inference, and also reduces memory usage and bandwidth demands. Precision reduction should be selectively applied to preserve the model accuracy.

#### **4.3. //**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

The GPU memory hierarchy is optimised for high throughput and low latency. For instance, the V100 integrated [shared memory]() (SMEM or SHM) and L1 cache, allowing L1 cache to take advantages of SMEM’s high-bandwidth performance and low-latency. In the A100, SMEM also supports rapid data exchange within the SM, and L1 cache stores frequently accessed data, both reducing the need for slower global memory access. The L2 cache, an off-chip memory shared across all SMs, enhances throughput to L1/SMEM by enabling quick access to larger datasets. It facilitates efficient data flow between SMs and the [high-bandwidth memory]() (HBM2). 

A high performance of HBM is essentially attributed to its vertical stacking architecture, i.e. multiple DRAM dies layered on top of each other and interconnected with [through-silicon vias]() (TSVs). However, an NVIDIA's [architect](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=n6M8R8-PlnE&t=75s) described, a primary bottleneck in GPU utilisation is memory bandwidth, not peak TFLOPS. Theoretically, the highest bandwidth of the A100 is 9,750 GB/s (64 bytes/SM × 108 SMs × 1410 MHz), yet HBM2's 1,555 GB/s is 6.3 times less than what the SMs require. Data access patterns pose a another challenge in utilisation; Developers should be aware of the contiguous memory allocation as CUDA (C for GPU) does not enforce it.

#### **4.4. //**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

An [interconnect]() is a communication link that enables data transfer between components within a computer system. In the context of GPUs and CPUs, interconnects allow different parts of a system—like processors, memory, storage devices, and expansion cards—to communicate efficiently. They can be physical connections like cables or circuit pathways on a motherboard, or even software protocols that manage how data flows between components. For example, technologies like NVIDIA’s [NVLink]() is an inter-GPU and GPU-CPU Interconnects, managing data transfer across various types of multiple processing units, and allowing GPUs to efficiently share data for large-scale parallel processing tasks. 

Whereas, system interconnects link the broader components of a system, such as processors, memory, storage, and expansion cards. Common interfaces like PCIe supports high-throughput connections essential for tasks that depend on rapid access to storage or network resources, maintaining data flow throughout the entire system.

## IV. Application
---
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

[Kaplan et al. (2020)]() proposed the "scaling laws for neural language models", and [Hoffmann et al. (2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.15556) expanded on this, suggesting that optimal compute utilisation in FLOPs is achieved by proportionally scaling both model parameters and training tokens, rather than simply increasing model size. They argued that larger LLMs, such as *GPT-3* and *Gopher*, were underfitted due to an insufficient amount of training data relative to their parameter counts. Consequently, they trained *Chinchilla* using the same compute budget as *Gopher* but with 1.4 trillion tokens (i.e. 4x bigger), outperforming many parameter-heavy models on most tasks, except mathematics.

<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/Vtu8Zbh3vBA-wAtHIZRKzQeZZBvAfnqLiSrCtK-SwLbs-bBRdH9YXyDRrAxxNrGDJ27XZ2B2wHRigZozDQJIVl0up_cZ9_mR7envmVh7QOS3SCrDGQ=w1232-rw" width="500" alt="https://irhum.github.io/blog/chinchilla/">

DeepMind's new training principles hyped smaller LLMs, inspired by *Transformer* introduced in [Vaswani et al. (2017)](), but inferencing at the production level needs further compute and memory optimisations, since they generate $K$ and $V$ autoregressively during the decoding. A [KV cache]() is specifically designed to reduce FLOPs by storing precomputed vectors in VRAM, but its size scales quickly with $\text{batch size}$ * $\text{context size}$ * $2$ * $\text{num. layers}$ * $\text{dim. hidden}$ * $\text{sizeof(precision)}$, restricting the use of large context sizes (e.g. ≥ 1M). For example, a *LLaMA-2 (7B)* loaded in FP16 and a batch size of 1 requires 14 GB for weights an additional 2 GB for cache.

<img src="https://arxiv.org/html/2312.05516v1/x1.png" width="500" alt="https://developer.nvidia.com/blog/mastering-llm-techniques-inference-optimization/">
<!-- https://developer-blogs.nvidia.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/key-value-caching_.png -->

Note that the input embeddings in the vanilla Transformer are projected into separate key-value pairs for each attention head $h_{i}$ within the MHA layer such that $h_{i} = \text{Attn}(Q_{i}, V_{i}, K_{i}) = \text{SM}(Q W_{i}^{(q)} (K W_{i}^{(k)})^T / d_{\text{head}}) V W_{i}^{(v)} \in \mathbb{R}^{\text{batch size} \,\times\, \text{seq. length} \,\times\, \text{dim. head}}$. MQA, by [Shazeer (2019)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.02150), takes a single set of mean-pooled key-value pairs for all $h_{i}$, lowering memory requirements for longer sequences at the cost of a potential degradation in model accuracy. [Ainslie et al. (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.13245), a group of fellows at Google, revisited MHA-MQA then proposed GQA, which offers greater flexibility in pairing the pairs while requiring some training from a checkpoint to adapt the model.

[Leviathan et al. (2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.17192) proposed [speculative decoding](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.17192), given the fact that language-modeling tasks often contain simpler subtasks, as an alternative to autoregressive logits processors (e.g. [beam search]()). It reduces inter-token latency (i.e. rather than TTFT) in memory-bound LLM inference by i) drafting: generate a speculative sequence with a smaller, faster auxiliary model; ii) verification: the original model evaluates drafts in parallel using causal self-attention, accepting only matching tokens and discarding mismatches; [Cai et al. In GPU computing, a kernel is a small, parallelisable program (i.e. code blocks) running on cores, with each launch incurring overhead such as memory transfers. [Kernel fusion]() lowers the overheads via consolidation of kernels into a single kernel, and [Dao et al. (2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.14135) proposed a fused I/O-aware attention that i) loads tiles of $Q, K, V$ from HBM to on-chip SRAM; ii) performs tile multiplications and incremental softmax reduction; and iii) obtains attention during the backward pass with a precomputed softmax normalising factor; Frameworks like PyTorch (i.e. automatic during compilation), CUDA, and TensorRT (i.e. for self-implementation) support kernel fusion.

Fine-tuning large models in commodity hardware is also challenging. LoRA, by [Hu et al. (2021)](), achieves this by updating only a small set of low-rank matrices, leaving the original model intact, with negligible extra memory and compute usages at inference. In contrast, [quantisation](https://huggingface.co/docs/transformers/main/en/quantization/overview) reduces memory by representing weights in lower precision that may slightly affect accuracy. [Dettmers et al. (2023)]() combined these in QLoRA, enhancing efficiency with (i) double quantisation for compact weight storage (e.g. 32 or 16 bits to 4 bits); (ii) the 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4) for lower quantisation error; and (iii) a paged optimiser to reduce VRAM use by offloading data to RAM;
