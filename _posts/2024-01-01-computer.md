## I
---
The [analytical engine]() (1837), designed by English mathematician Charles Babbage, is regarded as the concept of a general-purpose digital computer. The machine featured an arithmetic logic unit, control flow with conditional branching and loops, and memory modules. A [Turing machine]() (1936), proposed by Alan Turing, established a theoretical framework to analyse computational capabilities and limitations, laying the foundation for modern Computer Science. Before long, the [electronic numerical integrator and computer](), referred to as ENIAC (1945), designed by John Mauchly and J. Presper Eckert, became the first programmable, electronic, general-purpose digital computer.

While computers vary in size, processing power, and intended purposes, they generally fall into one of the following categories: i) [personal computers]() (PCs): extremely versatile and typically used by individuals for a wide range of office works, web browsing, gaming, multimedia, emulation, programming, and etc; ii) [mainframes]():  designed for high-volume transaction processing, bulk data handling, and mission-critical system that demand exceptional reliability and security; iii) [supercomputers](): deliver extraordinary processing speed and computational power, ideal for intensive tasks such as climate modeling, simulations, and intricate computations in fields like physics and cryptography.

The [motherboard]() inside a computer serves the physical circuitries and connections for three key components of [Von Neumann architecture]() proposed in 1945. This is the central hub where the CPU socket, RAM slots, storage interfaces, I/O ports, and other peripherals seamlessly communicate via the [buses]() and [chipsets](). The module in modern computers have expanded slots for GPUs, sound cards, or extra storages, intended to offer capabilities or flexibilities for future upgrades. The BIOS/UEFI [firmware]() embedded into the module first performs [self-tests]() (POST) as the power supplies, then the bootloader (e.g. Linux's GRUB, Windows's Boot Manager) loads the kernel from the SSD into RAM.

## II
---
### **1. Central Processing Unit**
<p style="margin-bottom: 10px;"> </p>

The [central processing unit]() (CPU or processor) executes a set of instructions, called a [program](), by reading and writing values in registers or memory, potentially altering their states. [Registers]() are the fastest memory, storing only the immediate instructions and data, and are located closest to the CPU's processing units. Given that each [core]() processes with [arithmetic logic unit]() (ALU) and [control unit]() (CU), and thereby the clock speed (GHz) and core count matter, modern CPUs hold between 4 to 64 cores for concurrent and parallel processing. Besides, a CPU also equips a multi-level [cache]()—L1 and L2 within each core, and shared L3—located closer to the cores, aiming to reduce memory latency.

### **2. Random Access Memory**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

A computer memory is basically an array of bytes each with a unique address. That is, an [random access memory]() (RAM), register, and cache can be viewed as unique abstractions. [Dynamic RAM]() (DRAM) latency depends on data access patterns and physical design of memory cells. Access is fastest when data is within an active row (i.e. contiguous), but latency increases by $T_{\text{precharge}} + T_{\text{row activation}} + T_{\text{column access}}$ as a new row must be accessed. [Static RAM]() (SRAM), known for its high speed and stability despite its higher cost, is therefore appropriate for cache. Notice that a portion of persistent storage could be allocated as [virtual memory]() when a process exceeds the memory limits of RAM.

### **3. Persistent Storage**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

Persistent storage includes [hard disk drives]() (HDDs) and [solid-state drives]() (SSDs), both non-volatile, retaining data without a power supply. HDDs are mechanical devices that read data through a process requiring physical movement to access an address, leading to slower access speeds. In contrast, SSDs utilise electronic signals, resulting in significantly faster access. In particular, many products released from 2016 onwards come equipped with [non-volatile memory express]() (NVMe) SSDs, in which NVMe operates over the [PCI Express]() (PCIe) interface to allow direct communication between the SSDs and the CPU, or, use flash memory that operates similarly to SSDs for storing data and Apps.

### **<a name="gpu"></a>4. Graphics Processing Unit**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

The [graphics processing unit]() (GPU), an integral of a graphics card, is specifically designed for [single instruction, multiple threads]() (SIMT) execution of relatively simple and repetitive tasks. Compared to CPUs, GPUs have significantly more cores, with each operatating at a lower clock speed, yet they ensure efficient computation and high throughput when paired with high-bandwidth memory. Confusingly, the similar terms [floating point operations per second]() (FLOPS) and [floating ooint operations]() (FLOPs) are used respectively to quantify a GPU’s performance and the computational demands of NNs. The latter clearly depends on hardware and model design (e.g. num. parameters).

Meanwhile, the [data processing unit]() (DPU), a newer programmable processor that is typically based on the [arm architecture](), is specifically designed to optimise data-centric tasks in large data centers, such as data movement, and storage (e.g. compression and encryption), and network packet processing (e.g. routing and security), rather than the compute-focused tasks. They offload data management from CPUs and orchestrate data flow between CPUs, GPUs, and storages. Notably, NVIDIA has introduced DPUs with robust security features, including isolation of data flows with hardware-level encryption and access control, which are critical for cloud-native and virtualised environments.

#### **4.2. //**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

The [streaming multiprocessor]() (SM) within a GPU is a core processing unit responsible for executing thread blocks. Given that a [thread]() is the smallest unit of execution that handles a portion of tensor operation (e.g. an element-wise multiplication), threads are organised into [warps]() of uniform size to execute instructions under the SIMT model, and a [thread block]() is a collection of warps assigned to each SM. One should dispatch more thread blocks than the number of SMs in modern GPUs to prevent the underutilisation of GPUs. When a core stalls, the [warp scheduler]() facilitates the switching between warps within the SMs, allowing for effective latency masking and maximising overall throughput.

A [cuda core]() within an SM is a a general-purpose processing units, essentially an ALU, performing basic arithmetic and logic operations such as $+$, $\times$, $\land$, $\lor$, and $\neg$. ... While each thread, with support for fp and int, generally handles one operation (e.g. $+$ or $\times$) per cycle, cuda cores are not yet optimal for matrix multiplication. Consequently, other neighboring hardware exists to remedy their limitations and increase overall throughput. ...

<img src="https://media.springernature.com/lw685/springer-static/image/chp%3A10.1007%2F978-981-15-6401-7_66-1/MediaObjects/491764_0_En_66-1_Fig9_HTML.jpg" width="500" alt="https://blog.paperspace.com/a-complete-anatomy-of-a-graphics-card-case-study-of-the-nvidia-a100/">

In 2007, NVIDIA introduced [tensor cores]() in its Volta and Turing architectures, designed specifically for efficient $4 \times 4$ matrix multiplications, called [fused multiply-add]() (FMA) operations, via [mixed precision]() per clock cycle. The input matrices for tensor cores are represented in fp16 (i.e. 2 bytes per element), which is half the precision of their outputs. This allows larger NNs to benefit from reduced memory requirements during both training and deployment, decreased memory bandwidth usage, and accelerated mathematical operations. Precision reduction should be applied selectively, targeting only the parts of the computation that do not necessitate full precision to preserve model quality.

#### **4.3. //**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

GPU memory modules maintain a hierarchy optimised for high throughput and low latency. Each SM in the A100 features 192 KB of [shared memory]() (SMEM), which is well-suited for large matrix multiplications and convolutions due to its high bandwidth and low latency. Meanwhile, the L1 cache reduces the need for slower global memory access by storing frequently used data. NVIDIA enhanced the performance of its V100 (the A100's predecessor) by integrating these memory types, allowing L1 cache operations to utilise SMEM advantages. Notably, CUDA programmers can take advantage of this optimization without complex memory management through APIs.

Off-chip and shared across SMs, the L2 cache enhances throughput to L1 /SMEM by providing quick access to larger datasets and facilitates data flow between SMs and the [high-bandwidth memory]() (HBM2). As emphasised by the [architect](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=n6M8R8-PlnE&t=75s), memory bandwidth is the bottleneck, not peak TFLOPS. Each A100 SM loads 64 bytes per clock cycle, yielding a theoretical bandwidth of 9750 GB/s (64 bytes × 108 SMs × 1410 MHz), while the HBM2 (aka, [VRAM]()) bandwidth is only 1555 GB/s, meaning cores request 6.3 times more bandwidth than available. In addition, matrices copied to cache via CPU may not be contiguous, but cuda (i.e. C for GPU) performs contiguous allocations in SMEM.

#### **4.4. //**
<p style="margin-bottom: 12px;"> </p>

An [interconnect]() is a communication link that enables data transfer between components within a computer system. In the context of GPUs and CPUs, interconnects allow different parts of a system—like processors, memory, storage devices, and expansion cards—to communicate efficiently. They can be physical connections like cables or circuit pathways on a motherboard, or even software protocols that manage how data flows between components. For example, technologies like NVIDIA’s [NVLink]() is an inter-GPU and GPU-CPU Interconnects, managing data transfer across various types of multiple processing units, and allowing GPUs to efficiently share data for large-scale parallel processing tasks. 

Whereas, system interconnects link the broader components of a system, such as processors, memory, storage, and expansion cards. Common interfaces like [peripheral component interconnect express]() (PCIe) supports high-throughput connections essential for tasks that depend on rapid access to storage or network resources, maintaining data flow throughout the entire system.
