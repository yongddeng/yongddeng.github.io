## I
---
A [probability theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_theory) is based on the paradigm of random [experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experiment_(probability_theory)) whose outcome cannot be predicted with certainty before an experiment is excuted. In reality, this experiment can be excuted repetedly under the same conditions in which the repetition is either in time or in space **(#1)**. Whilst probability theory concerns completely specified mathematical objects, [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics) concerns incompletely specified models which often has one or more (i.e. unknown) parameters, then collects, analyses, interprets, or even visualises samples to draw estimates for the given parameters. However, whether or not the repetition is more important than our prior knowledge highly depends on the characteristics of datasets and tasks.

In the subject, we call a set $\Omega$ a [sample space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sample_space), its element $\omega \in \Omega$ (i.e. resulted by random expriments) an [elementary event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_event), and a subset $A \subseteq \Omega$ such that $\lvert{A}\rvert \leq \lvert{\Omega}\rvert$ an [event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_(probability_theory)). In particular, we say $A$ is occured (not occured) if $\omega \in A$ ($\omega \notin A$). Subsequently, we define a [$\sigma$-algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%A3-algebra) $\mathcal{F} = \sigma(A)$ such that (i) $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$; (ii) if $A \in \mathcal{F}$, then $A^{c} \in \mathcal{F}$; (iii) if $A_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\bigcup_{n \in \mathbb{N}} A_n \in \mathcal{F}$; Note that there may exist one or more $\sigma$-algebras $\mathcal{F}^* = \{\mathcal{F}_0, \mathcal{F}_1, \dots \lvert \, A \subset \mathcal{F_n}\}$. We specifically say $\mathcal{F}$ is countably generated if there exists only countably many generators **(#2)**. Loosely speaking, any $\sigma$-algebra quantifies the amount of information available, and therefore, we search for the largest possible $\sigma$-algebra to make a tuple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$. 

The [power set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_set), denoted by $\mathcal{F}_{\Omega} = \sigma(\Omega)$, can be taken for a finite or a countable $\Omega$. It would however yield a sum of uncountably many probabilities $P(\Omega) = \Sigma_{\omega \in \Omega} p_{\omega} = \infty$ to violate the axiom, if we had uncountable $\Omega$.



However, when $\Omega$ is uncountable, it yields a sum of uncountably many probabilities $P(\Omega) = \Sigma_{\omega\in\Omega}{p_{\omega}} = \infty$ to violate the axiom. 
 
 To remedy this issue, whenever $\Omega = \mathbb{R}$, we expand the Euclidean space $(\mathbb{R}^n, \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}^n})$ by using the [Borel $\sigma$-algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borel_set) $\mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}^n} = \sigma(\mathcal{O} \subset \mathbb{R})$, where $\mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}^n}$ is the smallest $\sigma$-algebra containing every open subsets that are indeed measurable. In some cases, we use the monotone class theorem and the $\pi - \lambda$ theorem for an algebra and a $\pi$-system, respectively. For instance, if an algebra $A$ is a subset of a monotone class $\mathcal{M}$ **(#3)**, then the smallest $\sigma$-algebra $\sigma(A)$ is percisely equal to the smallest monotone class $\mathcal{M}(A)$.


## II
---
A [probability measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_measure) $P: \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]$ defined on some given $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ is a measure satisfying the [Kolmogorov axioms](https://www.stat.berkeley.edu/~aldous/Real_World/kolmogorov.html): (i) $P(A) \geq 0$ for any $A \in \mathcal{F}$; (ii) $P(\Omega) = 1$; (iii) $P(\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n) = \Sigma_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n)$ for pairwise disjoint (i.e. mutually exclusive) events $A_n$ **(#4)**. Persumably, a probability measure inherits useful properties of a measure including: (i) zero probability: $P\left(\emptyset\right) = 0$; (ii) complement: $P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$; (iii) monotonicity: if $A \subseteq B$, then $P(A) \leq P(B)$; (iv) sub-additivity: if $A \subseteq \bigcup_n A_n$, then $P(A) \leq \Sigma_n P(A_n)$; In particular, the monotonicity allows a probability measure to be well-defined on the limit of a sequence $(A_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ of events (i.e. if it exists). Note that it is easy to face nonempty sets that have zero probability.

If we properly assign a probability $p_\omega \geq 0$ for every $\omega \in \Omega$, making sure that $P\left(\Omega\right) = \Sigma_{\omega\in\Omega}\, p_{\omega} = 1$, then a probability of $A$ occuring is defined by $P(A) = \Sigma_{\omega\in{A}}\, p_\omega$. For instance, if we assign the [uniform probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_uniform_distribution) $p_\omega = {\lvert \Omega \rvert}^{-1}$ for some finite $\Omega$, then it is simply the counting measure. Instead, for a set of non-negative numbers $\Omega = \{k: k \in \mathbb{Z}^{\geq}\}$, and some pre-determined $\lambda > 0$ which refers an expected rate of occurences of some event in a fixed interval of time (or space), we can take the [Poisson probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_distribution) $p_k = {\lambda^k\over{k!}}e^{-\lambda}$ for the probability of $k$ occurences of an event. Whereas, for an uncountable sample space $\Omega=\mathbb{R}$, the most commonly shown probability measure is perhaps the [Gaussian probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_distribution) $f(x) = {1 \over \sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-{1 \over 2}({x - \mu \over \sigma})^2}$.

We shall call $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ a [probability space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_space) and also restrict it to a [complete probability space](https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/4095399/complete-probability-spaces) in order to preserve the measurability of null sets. If there exists $B \in \mathcal{F}$ such that $A \subset B$ with $P(B) = 0$ (so $B$ is measurable), then a set $A \subset \Omega$ that is not necessarily an element of $\mathcal{F}$ is a [null set](g/wiki/Null_set). The completion of space automatically consider that $A$ is measurable whenever $B$ is measurable. On the other hand, if $P(\omega) > 0$ and there exists no $\psi \in \Omega$ such that $P(\psi) > P(\omega)$, then we call $\omega \in \Omega$ an atom **(#5)**. This implies that an uncountable $\Omega$ can produce a [non-atomic proability space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom_(measure_theory)). I.e. the counting measure $\mu$ on $\Omega = \{1,2,\dots, 10\}$ takes a singleton $\{j\}$ as an atom, but the Lebesgue measure $\lambda$ on $\Omega = \mathbb{R}$ has no atom.


## III
---
Suppose $(A_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ and $A$ are subsets of $\Omega$. If $(A_n)_n\in\mathbb{N}$ is [strictly increasing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotonic_function) (strictly decreasing), denoted by $A_n \subset A_{n+1}$ ($A_n \supset A_{n+1}$), then the limit is given by $\lim_{n \to \infty} A_n = \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n$ ($\lim_{n \to \infty} A_n = \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n$). In addition, the limit of increasing unions of the initial segments $\bigcup_{k=1}^{n} A_k \subset \bigcup_{k=1}^{n+1} A_k$ is given by $\lim_{n \to \infty} \bigcup_{k=1}^{n} A_k = \bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty} A_k = A$, whereas the limit of decreasing unions of the tail segments $\bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k \supset \bigcup_{k=n+1}^{\infty} A_k$ is required to be $\lim_{n \to \infty} \bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k = \bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} \bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_k = \limsup_{n \to \infty} A_n = A^*$. Note that if we further set $\lim_{n\to\infty} \bigcap_{k=n}^{\infty}A = \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty}\bigcap_{k=n}^{\infty}A_k = \liminf_{n\to\infty}A_n = A_*$, and if, in addition, $A_* = A^*$, then $\lim_{n\to\infty}A_n = A_* = A^* = A$. In general, if we have $\lim_{n\to\infty}A_n = A$, then $A_* \subseteq A^*$.

A function usually has [continuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function) if both sides of limits are well-defined **(#6)**. Hence a probability measure along a [monotone sequence](http://www-groups.mcs.st-andrews.ac.uk/~john/analysis/Lectures/L8.html), that we had the limits, is clearly continuous. In other words, if $A_n \nearrow A$ ($A_n \searrow A$) as $n \to \infty$, then $P(A_n) \nearrow P(A)$ ($P(A_n) \searrow P(A)$) as $n \to \infty$, and we are free to let $\lim_{n\to\infty}P(A_n) = P(\lim_{n\to\infty}A_n) = P(A)$. Furthermore, based on the chain of inequalities $P\left( A_* \right) \leq \liminf_{n \to \infty} P\left( A_n \right) \leq \limsup_{n} P\left( A_n \right) \leq P(A^* )$, if $A_n \to A$ as $n \to \infty$, then $P\left( A_n \right) \to P(A)$ as $n \to \infty$. These interchange of a probability measure and a limit also well-behaves for the unions of segments, thus we have $\lim_{n \to \infty} P(\bigcup_{k=1}^{n} A_{k}) = P(\bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty} A_{k})$ and $\lim_{n \to \infty} P(\bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_{n}) = P(\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} \bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_{k})$.

The [Borel–Cantelli lemmas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borel%E2%80%93Cantelli_lemma) provide sufficient conditions for $(A_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ to have either the probability $0$ or $1$. Specifically, the 1st lemma states that if $\Sigma_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n) < \infty$, then almost all $\omega\in\Omega$ belong to at most a finite number of events $A_n$ because we have $P(A_n \text{ i.o.}) = P(\limsup_{n \to \infty} A_n) = \lim_{n\to\infty}P(\bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty}A_k) \leq \lim_{n\to\infty}\Sigma_{k=n}^{\infty}P(A_k) = 0$ **(#7)**. On the other hand, the 2nd lemma states that if $\Sigma_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n) = \infty$ and the events $A_n$ are ind., then it is true that $P(A_n \text{ i.o.}) = 1$. However, desptie of their usabilities in mathematical proofs, the [infinite monkey theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_monkey_theorem) which Émile Borel brought to the public encountered the pungent criticism and the 2nd lemma is considered to be quite nonsense in practice.
