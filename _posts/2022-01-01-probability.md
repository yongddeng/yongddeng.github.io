## I
---
A [probability theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_theory) is based on a paradigm of random [experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experiment_(probability_theory)) (i.e. trials) whose outcome cannot be predicted with certainty before an experiment is excuted. Such experiments in practice can be excuted repetedly under the same conditions in which the repetition is either in time or in space **(#1)**. Note that whether or not the repetition is more important than our prior knowledge depends on the types of datasets and tasks. A probability theory concerns completely specified mathematical objects in comparison with [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics) which concerns incomplete ones. In particular, we draw, analyse, and visualise samples to find an unbiased estimator of one or more unknown parameters for the latter.

Let a set $\Omega$ be a [sample space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sample_space), an element $\omega \in \Omega$ be an [elementary event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_event), and a subset $A \subseteq \Omega$ be an [event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_(probability_theory)) with a [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality) $\lvert{A}\rvert \leq \lvert{\Omega}\rvert$, thus we say $A$ is occured (not occured) if $\omega \in A$ ($\omega \notin A$). We construct a [$\sigma$-algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%A3-algebra) $\mathcal{F} = \sigma(A)$ such that (i) $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$; (ii) if $A \in \mathcal{F}$, then $A^{c} \in \mathcal{F}$; (iii) if $(A\_{n})\_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\bigcup_{n \in \mathbb{N}} A_n \in \mathcal{F}$; There may exist multiple $\sigma$-algebras $\mathcal{F}^* = \lbrace \mathcal{F}_0, \mathcal{F}_1, \dots \lvert \, A \subset \mathcal{F_n} \rbrace$, and we say $\mathcal{F}$ is countably generated if there exists only countably many generators **(#2)**. Loosely speaking, a $\sigma$-algebra generated by $A$ quantifies the amount of information that can be extracted from $A$, thus having the largest possible $\sigma$-algebra would result a more friutful tuple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$. 

The [power set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_set) $\mathcal{F}\_\Omega = \sigma(\Omega)$ can be taken for a finite or a countable $\Omega$. This however violates the below axiom if we had uncountable $\Omega$ as a sum of uncountably many probabilities $P(\Omega) = \Sigma\_{\omega\in\Omega} p\_\omega = \infty$. To remedy the issue, i.e. when $\Omega = \mathbb{R}$, we take the smallest $\sigma$-algebra containing every open subsets (that are indeed measurable), called the [Borel $\sigma$-algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borel_set) $\mathcal{B}\_{\mathbb{R}^n} = \sigma(\mathcal{O} \subset \mathbb{R})$, to build the Euclidean space $(\mathbb{R}^n, \mathcal{B}\_{\mathbb{R}^n})$. Besides, we can apply the monotone class theorem for an algebra, and the $\pi - \lambda$ theorem for a $\pi$-system. I.e. if an algebra $A$ is a subset of a monotone class $\mathcal{M}$ **(#3)**, then the smallest $\sigma$-algebra $\sigma(A)$ is percisely equal to the smallest monotone class $\mathcal{M}(A)$.


## II
---
A [probability measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_measure) $P: \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]$ defined on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ should satisfy the following [Kolmogorov axioms](https://www.stat.berkeley.edu/~aldous/Real_World/kolmogorov.html) (i) $P(A) \geq 0$ for all $A \in \mathcal{F}$; (ii) $P(\Omega) = 1$; (iii) if $(A\_{n})\_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ is a set of [pairwise disjoint events]() **(#4)**, then $P(\bigcup\_{n=1}^{\infty} A\_{n}) = \Sigma_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n)$; As expected, a probability measure inherits many useful properties of a measure including (i) zero probability: $P\left(\emptyset\right) = 0$; (ii) complement: $P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$; (iii) monotonicity: if $A \subseteq B$, then $P(A) \leq P(B)$; (iv) sub-additivity: if $A \subseteq \bigcup_n A_n$, then $P(A) \leq \Sigma_n P(A_n)$; and so on. Especially, the monotonicity allows $P$ to be well-defined on the limit of a sequence $(A_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ of events (i.e. if the limit exists). It is also fairly common to face non-empty sets that have zero probability.

If we properly assign a probability $p_\omega \geq 0$ for every $\omega \in \Omega$, making sure that $P\left(\Omega\right) = \Sigma_{\omega\in\Omega}\, p_{\omega} = 1$, then a probability of $A$ occuring is defined by $P(A) = \Sigma_{\omega\in{A}}\, p_\omega$. For instance, if we assign the [uniform probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_uniform_distribution) $p_\omega = {\lvert \Omega \rvert}^{-1}$ on some finite $\Omega$, then it is simply the counting measure. Instead, given a set of non-negative integers $\Omega = \lbrace k: k \in \mathbb{Z}^{\geq} \rbrace$ and an expected rate of occurences $\lambda > 0$ (of a particular event in a fixed time interval), we assign the [Poisson probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_distribution) $p_k = {\lambda^k\over{k!}}e^{-\lambda}$ as the probability of $k$ occurences of an event. Whereas, on the real number line $\Omega=\mathbb{R}$, we may assign the [Gaussian probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_distribution) $f(x) = {1 \over \sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-{1 \over 2}({x - \mu \over \sigma})^2}$ and hope for the best.

Finally, a triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is a [probability space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_space), and we shall restrict it to a [complete probability space](https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/4095399/complete-probability-spaces) in order to take the measurability of null sets. Namely, if there exists $B \in \mathcal{F}$ such that $A \subset B$ with $P(B) = 0$ (so $B$ is measurable), then a set $A \subset \Omega$ which may or may not be an element of $\mathcal{F}$ is a [null set](g/wiki/Null_set). The completion of space automatically considers $A$ as a measurable set whenever $B$ is measurable. On the other hand, whenever $P(\omega) > 0$, if there does not exists $\psi \in \Omega$ such that $P(\psi) > P(\omega)$. then we call $\omega \in \Omega$ an atom **(#5)**. It hints us that an uncountable $\Omega$ can make a [non-atomic proability space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom_(measure_theory)). I.e. the counting measure $\mu$ on $\Omega = \{1,2,\dots, 10\}$ takes a singleton $\{j\}$ as an atom, but the Lebesgue measure $\lambda$ on $\Omega = \mathbb{R}$ has no atom.


## III
---
Suppose $(A\_{n})\_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ and $A$ are subsets of $\Omega$. The limit of $(A\_{n})\_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ is given by $\lim\_{n \to \infty} A\_{n} = \bigcup\_{n=1}^{\infty} A\_{n}$ ($\lim\_{n \to \infty} A\_n = \bigcap\_{n=1}^{\infty} A\_{n}$) if it is [strictly increasing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotonic_function) (strictly decreasing), i.e. $A\_{n} \subset A\_{n+1}$ ($A\_{n} \supset A\_{n+1}$). Furthermore, the limit of increasing unions of the initial segments $\bigcup\_{k=1}^{n} A\_{k} \subset \bigcup\_{k=1}^{n+1} A\_k$ is given by $\lim\_{n \to \infty} \bigcup\_{k=1}^{n} A\_k = \bigcup\_{k=1}^{\infty} A\_k = A$, and the limit of decreasing unions of the tail segments $\bigcup\_{k=n}^{\infty} A\_{k} \supset \bigcup\_{k=n+1}^{\infty} A\_{k}$ is given by $\lim\_{n\to\infty} \bigcup\_{k=n}^{\infty} A\_{k} = \bigcap\_{n=1}^{\infty} \bigcup\_{k=n}^{\infty} A\_{k} = \limsup_{n\to\infty} A\_{n} = A^{\ast}$. Similarly, we get $\lim\_{n\to\infty} \bigcap\_{k=n}^{\infty}A = \bigcup\_{n=1}^{\infty}\bigcap\_{k=n}^{\infty} A\_{k} = \liminf\_{n\to\infty} A\_{n} = A\_{\ast}$, and if $A\_{\ast} = A^{\ast}$, then $\lim\_{n\to\infty} A\_{n} = A\_{\ast} = A^{\ast} = A$. Note that, if one has $\lim\_{n\to\infty} A\_{n} = A$, then usually $A\_{\ast} \subseteq A^{\ast}$.

As a real-valued function with well-defined limits in both directions usually has [continuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function) **(#6)**, a probability measure along a [monotone sequence](http://www-groups.mcs.st-andrews.ac.uk/~john/analysis/Lectures/L8.html) is continuous. Consequently, if $A_n \nearrow A$ ($A_n \searrow A$) as $n \to \infty$, then $P(A_n) \nearrow P(A)$ ($P(A_n) \searrow P(A)$) as $n \to \infty$, and we are free to write $\lim_{n\to\infty}P(A_n) = P(\lim_{n\to\infty}A_n) = P(A)$. In particular, based on the chain of probabilistic inequalities $P\left( A_\ast \right) \leq \liminf_{n \to \infty} P\left( A_n \right) \leq \limsup_{n} P\left( A_n \right) \leq P(A^\ast )$, if $A_n \to A$ as $n \to \infty$, then $P\left( A_n \right) \to P(A)$ as $n \to \infty$. The interchange of a probability measure and a limit also works for the unions, thus we let $\lim_{n \to \infty} P(\bigcup_{k=1}^{n} A_{k}) = P(\bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty} A_{k})$ and $\lim_{n \to \infty} P(\bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_{n}) = P(\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} \bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_{k})$.

The [Borel–Cantelli lemmas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borel%E2%80%93Cantelli_lemma) provide sufficient conditions for a sequence $(A\_n)\_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ to have either the probability $0$ or $1$ (i.e. thus it converges). Specifically, the 1st lemma states that if $\Sigma\_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n) < \infty$, then almost all $\omega\in\Omega$ belong to at most a finite number of events $A_n$ since we have $P(A_n \text{ i.o.}) = P(\limsup\_{n \to \infty} A_n) = \lim_{n\to\infty}P(\bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A\_k) \leq \lim_{n\to\infty}\Sigma_{k=n}^{\infty}P(A\_k) = 0$ **(#7)**. On the other hand, the 2nd lemma states that if $\Sigma_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A\_n) = \infty$ and the events $A_n$ are ind., then it is true that $P(A_n \text{ i.o.}) = 1$. However, desptie of their usabilities in mathematical proofs, the [infinite monkey theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_monkey_theorem) which Émile Borel brought to the public encountered the pungent criticism and the 2nd lemma is considered to be quite nonsense in practice.
