## I
---
A [probability theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_theory) is based on a paradigm of random [experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experiment_(probability_theory)) (i.e. trials) whose outcome cannot be predicted with certainty before an experiment is excuted. Such experiments in practice can be excuted repetedly under the same conditions in which the repetition is either in time or in space **(#1)**. Note that whether or not the repetition is more important than our prior knowledge depends on the types of datasets and tasks. A probability theory concerns completely specified mathematical objects in comparison with [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics) which concerns incomplete ones. In particular, we draw, analyse, and visualise samples to find an unbiased estimator of one or more unknown parameters for the latter.

Let a set $\Omega$ be a [sample space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sample_space), an element $\omega \in \Omega$ be an [elementary event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_event), and a subset $A \subseteq \Omega$ be an [event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_(probability_theory)) with a cardinality $\lvert{A}\rvert \leq \lvert{\Omega}\rvert$, thus $A$ is occurred (not occurred) if $\omega \in A$ ($\omega \notin A$). We can construct a [$\sigma$-algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%A3-algebra) $\mathcal{F} = \sigma(A)$ such that (i) $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$; (ii) if $A \in \mathcal{F}$, then $A^{c} \in \mathcal{F}$; (iii) if $(A\_{n})\_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\bigcup_{n \in \mathbb{N}} A_n \in \mathcal{F}$; There may exist multiple $\sigma$-algebras $\mathcal{F}^* = \lbrace \mathcal{F}_0, \mathcal{F}_1, \dots \lvert \, A \subset \mathcal{F_n} \rbrace$, and we say $\mathcal{F}$ is countably generated if there exists only countably many generators **(#2)**. Loosely speaking, a $\sigma$-algebra generated by $A$ quantifies the amount of information that can be extracted from $A$, thus having the largest possible $\sigma$-algebra would result a more friutful tuple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$. 

The [power set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_set) $\mathcal{F}\_\Omega = \sigma(\Omega)$ can be taken for a finite or a countable $\Omega$. This however violates the below axiom if we had uncountable $\Omega$ as a sum of uncountably many probabilities $P(\Omega) = \Sigma\_{\omega\in\Omega} p\_\omega = \infty$. To remedy the issue (i.e. $\Omega = \mathbb{R}$), we usually take the smallest $\sigma$-algebra containing every open subsets (i.e. that are indeed measurable), called the [Borel $\sigma$-algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borel_set) $\mathcal{B}\_{\mathbb{R}} = \sigma(\mathcal{O} \subset \mathbb{R})$, to build the Euclidean space $(\mathbb{R}^n, \mathcal{B}\_{\mathbb{R}^n})$. Besides, we can apply the monotone class theorem for an algebra, and the $\pi - \lambda$ theorem for a $\pi$-system. That is, if an algebra $A$ is a subset of a monotone class $\mathcal{M}$ **(#3)**, then the smallest $\sigma$-algebra $\sigma(A)$ is percisely equal to the smallest monotone class $\mathcal{M}(A)$.


## II
---
A [probability measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_measure) $P: \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]$ defined on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ should satisfy the following [Kolmogorov axioms](https://www.stat.berkeley.edu/~aldous/Real_World/kolmogorov.html) (i) $P(A) \geq 0$ for any $A \in \mathcal{F}$; (ii) $P(\Omega) = 1$; (iii) if $(A\_{n})\_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ is a set of pairwise disjoint events **(#4)**, then $P(\bigcup\_{n=1}^{\infty} A\_{n}) = \Sigma_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n)$; As expected, it inherits many useful properties of a measure such as (i) zero probability: $P\left(\emptyset\right) = 0$; (ii) complement: $P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$; (iii) monotonicity: if $A \subseteq B$, then $P(A) \leq P(B)$; (iv) sub-additivity: if $A \subseteq \bigcup_n A_n$, then $P(A) \leq \Sigma_n P(A_n)$; and so on. In fact, the monotonicity allows $P$ to be well-defined on the limits (i.e. if exists) of a sequence $(A_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ of events. It is also common to obtain non-empty sets with zero probability.

If we properly assign a probability $p_\omega \geq 0$ for every $\omega \in \Omega$, making sure that $P\left(\Omega\right) = \Sigma_{\omega\in\Omega}\, p_{\omega} = 1$, then a probability of $A$ occurring is defined by $P(A) = \Sigma_{\omega\in{A}}\, p_\omega$. For instance, if we assign the [uniform probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_uniform_distribution) $p_\omega = {\lvert \Omega \rvert}^{-1}$ on some finite $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}$, then $P$ will behave simply as the counting measure. Whereas, the [Gaussian probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_distribution) $f(x) = {1 \over \sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-{1 \over 2}({x - \mu \over \sigma})^2}$ draws a more interesting shape which can also describe the nature. In discrete sense, given a set of non-negative integers $\Omega = \lbrace k: k \in \mathbb{N}\_{0} \rbrace$ and an expected rate of occurrences $\lambda > 0$, the [Poisson probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_distribution) $p_k = {\lambda^k\over{k!}}e^{-\lambda}$, which can be derived from the [binomial distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_distribution), assigns measures to an event occurring $k$ times.

A triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$, which we call a [probability space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_space), shall be restricted to a [complete probability space](https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/4095399/complete-probability-spaces) for the measurability of null sets. If there exists $B \in \mathcal{F}$ such that $A \subset B$ with $P(B) = 0$ (i.e. $B$ is measurable), then $A \subset \Omega$ which may or may not be an element of $\mathcal{F}$ is a [null set](g/wiki/Null_set), and here the completion treats $A$ being measurable whenever $B$ is measurable. On the other hand, if there does not exists $\psi \in \Omega$ such that $P(\psi) > P(\omega) > 0$, then $\omega \in \Omega$ is an atom **(#5)**. It hints us that an uncountable $\Omega$ can make a [non-atomic proability space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom_(measure_theory)). I.e. the counting measure $\mu$ on $\Omega = \lbrace 1,2,\dots, 10 \rbrace$ takes a singleton $\lbrace j \rbrace$ as an atom, but the Lebesgue measure $\lambda$ on $\Omega = \mathbb{R}$ has no atom.


## III
---
Suppose $(A\_{n})\_{n \in \mathbb{N}}$ and $A$ are subsets of $\Omega$. If $(A\_{n})\_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ is [strictly increasing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotonic_function) (strictly decreasing), that is, $A\_{n} \subset A\_{n+1}$ ($A\_{n} \supset A\_{n+1}$), then $\lim\_{n \to \infty} A\_{n} = \bigcup\_{n=1}^{\infty} A\_{n}$ ($\lim\_{n \to \infty} A\_n = \bigcap\_{n=1}^{\infty} A\_{n}$). Also, the limit of increasing unions of the initial segments $\bigcup\_{k=1}^{n} A\_{k} \subset \bigcup\_{k=1}^{n+1} A\_k$ is given by $\lim\_{n \to \infty} \bigcup\_{k=1}^{n} A\_k = \bigcup\_{k=1}^{\infty} A\_k$, and the limit of decreasing unions of the tail segments $\bigcup\_{k=n}^{\infty} A\_{k} \supset \bigcup\_{k=n+1}^{\infty} A\_{k}$ is given by $\lim\_{n\to\infty} \bigcup\_{k=n}^{\infty} A\_{k} = \bigcap\_{n=1}^{\infty} \bigcup\_{k=n}^{\infty} A\_{k} = \limsup_{n\to\infty} A\_{n}$. If $\lim\_{n\to\infty} \bigcap\_{k=n}^{\infty}A\_{k} = \bigcup\_{n=1}^{\infty}\bigcap\_{k=n}^{\infty} A\_{k} = \liminf\_{n\to\infty} A\_{n}$ is equal to $\limsup_{n\to\infty} A\_{n}$, then $\liminf_{n\to\infty} A\_{n} = \limsup_{n\to\infty} A\_{n} = \lim\_{n\to\infty} A\_{n} = A$. Note that the first equality is not followed in general.

Provided that, $P$ along with a [monotone sequence](http://www-groups.mcs.st-andrews.ac.uk/~john/analysis/Lectures/L8.html) is continuous as a real-valued function with well-defined limits often has [continuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function) **(#6)**. That is, if $A_n \nearrow A$ ($A_n \searrow A$) as $n \to \infty$, then $P(A_n) \nearrow P(A)$ ($P(A_n) \searrow P(A)$) as $n \to \infty$. In particular, based on the chain of probabilistic inequalities $P\left(\liminf\_{n \to \infty}A\_{n}\right) \leq \liminf\_{n \to \infty} P\left(A\_{n} \right) \leq \limsup\_{n \to \infty} P\left(A\_{n} \right) \leq P\left(\limsup\_{n \to \infty} A\_{n}\right)$, if $A\_{n} \to A$ as $n \to \infty$, then $P\left(A\_{n} \right) \to P(A)$ as $n \to \infty$. Moreover, the interchange of a probability measure and a limit of a union is allowed, thus we can denote $\lim_{n \to \infty} P(\bigcup_{k=1}^{n} A_{k}) = P(\bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty} A_{k})$, and $\lim_{n \to \infty} P(\bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_{n}) = P(\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} \bigcup_{k=n}^{\infty} A_{k}) = P(\limsup_{n\to\infty} A\_{n})$.

The [Borel–Cantelli lemmas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borel%E2%80%93Cantelli_lemma) state sufficient conditions for a convergence of a sequence $(A\_n)\_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ of events. Specifically, the 1st lemma states that, if $\Sigma\_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n) < \infty$, then $P(A_n \text{ i.o.}) = P(\limsup\_{n \to \infty} A_n) \leq \lim_{n\to\infty}\Sigma_{k=n}^{\infty}P(A\_k) = 0$ **(#7)**, signifying that almost every $\omega\in\Omega$ belong to at most a finite number of events $A_n$. Whereas, the 2nd lemma states that, if $\Sigma_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A\_n) = \infty$ and the events $A_n$ are independent, then $P(A_n \text{ i.o.}) = 1$. However, desptie of their usabilities in mathematical proofs, the [infinite monkey theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_monkey_theorem) which Émile Borel brought to the public encountered the pungent criticism, and so the 2nd lemma has been considered to be quite nonsense in practice.
