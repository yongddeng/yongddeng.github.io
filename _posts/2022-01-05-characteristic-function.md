# Characteristic Function
---
The [convolution theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convolution_theorem) puts $\operatorname{FT} \lbrace f*g \rbrace = \operatorname{FT} \lbrace f \rbrace \cdot \operatorname{FT} \lbrace g \rbrace$, where $\operatorname{FT}$ and $(f*g)(x) = \int\_{\mathbb{R}} f(\tau)g(x-\tau)\,\mathrm{d}\tau$ denotes the Fourier transform and convolution, respectively. 

Therefore, convolution of $f,g\in\mathbb{R}$ can be computed by multiplying $\hat{f},\hat{g}\in\mathbb{C}$ and taking $\operatorname{FT}^{-1}$. The characteristic function in general is the probabilistic version of $\operatorname{FT}$ that is applied to the density function $f\_{Z}(z) = \int\_{x \in X} f\_{X}(x) f\_{Y}(z-x)\, \mathrm{d}x$, where $Z = X+Y$ and $f\_{X,Y} = f\_X f\_Y$.


## I
---
Suppose $X:\Omega\to\mathbb{R}$ is a random variable. The [characteristic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_function_(probability_theory)) (cf) $\varphi_X: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{C}$ is provided by $\varphi_X(t) = \operatorname{E}e^{itX}$, where $i$ is the imaginary unit and $t \in \mathbb{R}$ is the argument. Clearly, if $X$ admits the density $f_X$, then the definition $\varphi_X(t) = \int_{\mathbb{R}} e^{itx}f_X(x)\,\mathrm{d}x$ equals to the [Fourier transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_transform) of $f_X$ with sign reversal in the exponent **(#1)**. In particular, if $Y = aX + b$, then $\varphi_Y(t)= e^{itb}\varphi_{X}(at)$. Furthermore, for a sequence $(X_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ of ind. random variables, if we let $S_n = \Sigma_{k=1}^{n} a_kX_k$, then $\varphi_{S_n}(t) = \prod_{k=1}^{n} \varphi_{X_k}(a_{k}t)$ due to the linearity $\operatorname{E}e^{it(aX+b)} = \operatorname{E}e^{itaX}e^{itb} = e^{itb}\operatorname{E}e^{itaX}$ and the multiplicativity $\operatorname{E}e^{it(X_1 + X_2 + \dots{+X_n})} = \operatorname{E}\prod_{k=1}^{n}e^{itX_k} = \prod_{k=1}^{n}\operatorname{E}e^{itX_k}$; Similar definitions can be followed for a a random matrix $X_{m,n} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$.

To prove the existence of $\varphi_X$, we recall Jensen's inequality and set ${\vert \varphi_X(t) \vert} = {\vert \operatorname{E}e^{itX} \vert} \leq \operatorname{E}{\vert e^{itX} \vert}$. Subsequently, because ${\vert e^{itX} \vert} = {\vert \cos^2(tx)+i\sin^2(tx) \vert} = [\cos^2(tx)+\sin^2(tx)]^{1/2}=1$ and $P(\Omega) = 1$, we can bound ${\vert \varphi_X(t) \vert} \leq 1$, and so the integral is finite as the exponential function is bounded and the measure of space is finite. Although the integral dose not vanish around $0$ and $\varphi_X(0) = 1$, the [Riemann–Lebesgue lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann%E2%80%93Lebesgue_lemma) states that the $\operatorname{FT}$ of $L^1$-functions vanish at $\plusmn\infty$, and hence $\lim_{t \to \plusmn\infty} {\vert \varphi_X(t) \vert} = 0$. On the other hand, to prove the uniform continuity of $\varphi_X$, we can show that the estimate of difference $\vert \varphi_X(t+h)-\varphi_X(t) \vert$ is bounded by an arbitrarily $\varepsilon > 0$ for any value $h > 0$ whilst the estimate does not depend on $t\in\mathbb{R}$.

By definition, for any random variable $X$, one has $m_{X}(t) = \varphi_{X}(-it)$. Furthermore, a complex-valued function $g$ is Hermitian if and only if $\operatorname{FT}\{g\}$ is real-valued. Then, by construction, $\varphi_X$ is Hermitian as $f_X = \operatorname{FT}^{-1}\{\varphi_X\}$ is real-valued, and thus $\varphi^{*}_X(t) = \varphi_{X}(-t)$, where $*$ denotes complex conjugate. In particular, $\varphi_X$ of a symmetric random variable $X$ is real-valued and even in which $\varphi_{X}(-t) = \varphi_{-X}(t)$. It is fairly obvious that $F_X=F_Y$ if and only if $\varphi_X = \varphi_Y$. In fact, the [Lévy's continuity theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9vy%27s_continuity_theorem) states that a bijection between $F_X$ and $\varphi_X$ is sequentially continuous, and thus given any sequence $(X_n)_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ **(#2)**, if $\varphi_{X_n} \to \varphi_X$ pointwisely as $n \to \infty$ (i.e. for all $t\in\mathbb{R}$), then one can conclude that $F_{X_n} \to F_{X}$ as $n\to\infty$.


## II
---
Due to the continuity theorem, $\varphi_X$ can easily be found in the proof of theorems related to the convergence (in distribution) of random variables as well as sums of random variables (i.e. the law of large numbers and the central limit theorem). Yet, we need to explore the features that have been founded in the study of Fourier transform, especially the reciprocal relationship between the Fourier transform and the [inverse Fourier transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_inversion_theorem) is necessary. Suppose $\varphi_X$ is integrable and $F_X$ is continuous. The inversion formula $f_X(x) = {1 \over 2\pi} \int_{\mathbb{R}} e^{-itx} \varphi_X(t)\,\mathrm{d}t$ provides the density function of $X$ beased on $\varphi_X$, or, equivalently, this returns the bijective Radon-Nikodym derivative $f_X$ computed with respect to a probability measure $P$.

Paul Lévy states that $F_X(b) - F_X(a) + {1\over2}P(X=a)-{1\over2}P(X=b) = \lim_{T\to\infty} {1\over2\pi} \int_{-T}^{T} {e^{-itb} - e^{-ita} \over it} \varphi(t)\, dt$, where $a,b\in\mathbb{R}$ and $a < b$. This allows us to inverse $\operatorname{FT}$ for both discrete and continuous random variable $X$. For example, the pmf vanishes if $a$ and $b$ are contained in the [continuity set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_set) $C(F_X) = \{x: F_X(x) \text{ is continuous at } x\}$, and so it is simplified by $F_X(b) - F_X(a) = \lim_{T\to\infty} {1\over2\pi} \int_{-T}^{T} {e^{-itb} - e^{-ita} \over it} \varphi(t)\, dt$. The equation may be resated by ${F(x+h)-F(x-h)\over{2h}} = {1\over{2\pi}}\int_{\mathbb{R}} {\sin{ht}\over{ht}}e^{-itx}\varphi_X(t)\, dt$ for advantages in numerical computations. Although these formulas only demonstrate the univariate case, we can extend them for the multivariate case by calculating the chain of integrals with respect to $P$ that lies on $\mathbb{R}^n$.

Furthermore, J. Gil-Pelaez states that $F_X(x) = {1\over{2}} + {1\over{2\pi}} \int_{0}^{\infty} {e^{itx}\varphi(-t) - e^{-itx}\varphi(t) \over it}\, \mathrm{d}t = {1\over{2}} - {1\over{2\pi}} \int_{0}^{\infty} {\operatorname{Im}[e^{-itx}\varphi_X(t)] \over t}\, \mathrm{d}t$, where $x \in \mathbb{R}$ is a continuity point of $F_X$ **(#3)**. ____NotReadThePaperYet


## III
---
The practical uses of characteristic function can also be found in the modern machine learning era. For instance, Google Research developed the [Performer (2020)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2009.14794), a new Transformer architecture which equipped linearly scalable attention mechanisms and enabled faster training over longer sequences, based on the [Random Features (2007)](https://people.eecs.berkeley.edu/~brecht/papers/07.rah.rec.nips.pdf) which proposed to reduce the computational cost of inner product by applying the Bochner's theorem on $k(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{y}) = \phi(\boldsymbol{x})^{\top} \phi(\boldsymbol{y})$, where $k:\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is a kernel, $\phi:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$ is a feature map, and $m \geq n$ **(#4)**. The authors attempted to replace the attention matrix by continuous positive-definite $k$ being approximated with the randomized feature map $z: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$, where $m \leq n$.

Specifically, for any $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n \in \mathbb{R}^n$, if one has a [positive-semidefinite matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Definite_matrix) $A_{n \times n} = (a_{qr})_{q,r=1}^{n}$ with $a_{qr}=g(x_q - x_r)$, then $g:\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{C}$ is a [positive-definite function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive-definite_function). In fact, the positive-definiteness of $g$ arises whenever there exists $f \geq 0$ such that $g = \operatorname{FT}\{f\}$. Therefore, if one has a continuous positive-definite function $\phi: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{C}$ with $\phi(0) = 1$, then there always exists a Borel probability measure $\mu:\mathbb{R^n} \to [0,1]$ such that $\phi = \operatorname{FT}\{\mu\}$. This particular result, known as the [Bochner's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bochner%27s_theorem), tells us that $p(\omega) = {1\over{2\pi}}\int_{\mathbb{R}} e^{-i\omega^{\top}(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{y})} k(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{y})\, \mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{\triangle}$ is a proper distribution function, if the shift-invariant kernel $k(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{y})=k(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{y})$ in the integral has been properly scaled and so $k(0) = 1$.

We immediately use the inversion formula to revert $k(\boldsymbol{x} − \boldsymbol{y}) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} e^{i\omega^{\top}(\boldsymbol{x-\boldsymbol{y}})}p(\omega)\, \mathrm{d}\omega$ and also restate $k(\boldsymbol{x} − \boldsymbol{y}) = \operatorname{E}[\zeta_\omega(\boldsymbol{x})\zeta_\omega(\boldsymbol{y})^*]$ with $\zeta_\omega(\boldsymbol{x}) = e^{i\omega^{\top}\boldsymbol{x}}$. Hence $\zeta_\omega(\boldsymbol{x})\zeta_\omega(\boldsymbol{y})^*$ is an unbiased estimate of $k(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{y})$ when $\omega$ is drawn from $p(\omega)$. Subsequently, if $p(\omega)$ and $k(\triangle)$ are real, the integral converges when $e^{\boldsymbol{x}} = \cos(\boldsymbol{x}) + i\sin(\boldsymbol{x})$ is replaced by $\cos(\boldsymbol{x})$, and thus we have $k(\boldsymbol{x},\boldsymbol{y})=\operatorname{E}[z_\omega(\boldsymbol{x})z_\omega(\boldsymbol{y})]$, where $z_\omega(\boldsymbol{x}) = \sqrt{2}\cos(\omega^{\top}\boldsymbol{x} + b)$ and $b$ is drawn uniformly from $[0,2\pi]$. That is, we substitute $k(\boldsymbol{x}−\boldsymbol{y}) \approx z(\boldsymbol{x})^{\top}z(\boldsymbol{y})$ when $z$ can map data point onto a lower-dimensional feature space with small variances **(#5)**. Note that this can be used for any inner product operations that are frequent in ML (i.e. OLS, SVM, PCA, etc).
